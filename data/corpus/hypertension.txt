Hypertension: Clinical Management Guidelines

Hypertension, or high blood pressure, is defined as systolic blood pressure ≥140 mmHg or diastolic blood pressure ≥90 mmHg. It is a major risk factor for cardiovascular disease, stroke, and chronic kidney disease.

Classification:
- Normal: <120/80 mmHg
- Elevated: 120-129/<80 mmHg
- Stage 1 Hypertension: 130-139/80-89 mmHg
- Stage 2 Hypertension: ≥140/90 mmHg
- Hypertensive Crisis: >180/120 mmHg

First-Line Antihypertensive Agents:
1. ACE Inhibitors (e.g., lisinopril, enalapril)
2. Angiotensin Receptor Blockers (ARBs) (e.g., losartan, valsartan)
3. Calcium Channel Blockers (e.g., amlodipine, diltiazem)
4. Thiazide Diuretics (e.g., hydrochlorothiazide, chlorthalidone)

Treatment Goals:
- General population: <130/80 mmHg
- Elderly (≥65 years): <130/80 mmHg (if tolerated)
- Diabetes or CKD: <130/80 mmHg

Lifestyle Modifications:
- DASH diet (Dietary Approaches to Stop Hypertension)
- Sodium restriction (<2.3 g/day, ideally <1.5 g/day)
- Regular physical activity (150 min/week moderate intensity)
- Weight loss if overweight (target BMI 18.5-24.9)
- Limit alcohol consumption
- Smoking cessation

Monitoring:
- Home blood pressure monitoring recommended
- Office BP measurements at each visit
- Annual assessment of cardiovascular risk factors
- Renal function and electrolytes when starting or adjusting medications

Resistant Hypertension:
Defined as BP above goal despite adherence to ≥3 antihypertensive medications of different classes, including a diuretic. Consider secondary causes and refer to specialist.

Secondary Causes to Consider:
- Primary aldosteronism
- Renal artery stenosis
- Obstructive sleep apnea
- Pheochromocytoma
- Cushing's syndrome
- Thyroid disorders
