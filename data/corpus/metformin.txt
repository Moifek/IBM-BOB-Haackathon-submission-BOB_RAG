Metformin: Clinical Overview

Metformin is a first-line medication for the treatment of type 2 diabetes mellitus. It belongs to the biguanide class of antidiabetic drugs and works primarily by decreasing hepatic glucose production and improving insulin sensitivity in peripheral tissues.

Mechanism of Action:
Metformin activates AMP-activated protein kinase (AMPK), which leads to decreased gluconeogenesis in the liver. It also enhances glucose uptake in skeletal muscle and reduces intestinal glucose absorption.

Clinical Indications:
- Type 2 diabetes mellitus (first-line therapy)
- Polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS)
- Prediabetes prevention
- Gestational diabetes (off-label)

Dosing:
Initial dose: 500 mg twice daily or 850 mg once daily with meals
Maintenance dose: 1000-2000 mg daily in divided doses
Maximum dose: 2550 mg daily

Common Side Effects:
- Gastrointestinal disturbances (diarrhea, nausea, abdominal discomfort)
- Metallic taste
- Vitamin B12 deficiency (with long-term use)

Contraindications:
- Severe renal impairment (eGFR <30 mL/min/1.73m²)
- Acute or chronic metabolic acidosis
- Severe hepatic impairment
- Acute heart failure
- Recent myocardial infarction

Drug Interactions:
Metformin may interact with contrast media, alcohol, and certain medications that affect renal function. Caution is advised when combining with other antidiabetic agents due to increased hypoglycemia risk.

Monitoring:
- Renal function (eGFR) at baseline and periodically
- Vitamin B12 levels annually
- HbA1c every 3-6 months
- Liver function tests if clinically indicated
